\chapter{Assumptions, Scope, and Failure Coverage}
\label{ch:assumptions-scope-failure-coverage}

\begin{chapterthesis}
The framework applies only if civilization retains enough epistemic, institutional, and physical capacity to evaluate and constrain frontier AI systems before irreversible capability growth; broader pre-ASI risks are treated as assumption threats rather than the central object of the book.
\end{chapterthesis}

\section{Why This Matters}

Superintelligence alignment is the problem of preserving human-correctable value-bearing processes across capability growth, ontology shift, successor creation, and strategic multi-agent selection pressure---under the assumption that civilization still has enough correction capacity to participate in the process.

This book does not attempt a general taxonomy of all ways advanced AI systems can fail.

\section{Scope Statement}

\subsection{In scope}

\begin{itemize}
  \item wrong optimizer / wrong boundary
  \item capability growth
  \item ontology shift
  \item successor creation
  \item value-bundle transport
  \item bearer-map preservation
  \item correction-channel integrity
  \item multi-agent strategic coupling
  \item socio-technical attractor basins
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Background assumptions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item civilization has not already lost correction capacity
  \item frontier systems remain observable enough for measurement
  \item deployment can still be conditioned on certification
  \item humans retain enough agency to refuse, revise, and coordinate
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Out of scope}

\begin{itemize}
  \item ordinary bugs and cyber failures
  \item hardware accidents
  \item general bio/military misuse
  \item late cosmic optimization pathologies
  \item pure opportunity-cost arguments
  \item AI halting except as a successor-certification footnote
\end{itemize}

Bugs, cybersecurity failures, hardware faults, viruses, infrastructure failures, and late cosmic-stage pathologies matter, but they are not the target of this theory except where they alter agency boundaries, successor stability, value-bundle transport, or correction-channel integrity.

\section{Correction-Capacity Assumption}

\textbf{[Defined]} Let $C_{\text{corr}}^{\text{society}}(t_0)$ denote society's institutional, epistemic, and practical capacity to notice, evaluate, and constrain frontier systems at time $t_0$.
The theory assumes
\[
C_{\text{corr}}^{\text{society}}(t_0) > \theta
\]
for some threshold $\theta$.

The theory begins after society still has enough institutional, epistemic, and practical capacity to notice, evaluate, and constrain frontier systems.
If that capacity has already collapsed, the later alignment theory may arrive too late.
