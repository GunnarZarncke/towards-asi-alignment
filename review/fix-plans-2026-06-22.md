## Strategy vocabulary (used in §A and §B)

For each duplication we pick one of your eight strategies. To keep judgments comparable:

| Strategy | What it means in the manuscript | Best when |
|---|---|---|
| **elide** | Delete the restatement entirely; no pointer. | The local passage reads fine without it; the object is incidental here. |
| **use-as-known** | Refer to the object by name, no restatement, no explicit ref. | Object is central and recent; reader cannot have missed it. |
| **simple-reference** | Replace restatement with `(Eq.~X, Ch.~Y)`. | Reader may need to look it up but not be reminded inline. |
| **reminder** | One-line recap + reference: "recall \(G_B\) (Eq.~X) measures…". | Object recurs after distance; a 1-line jog preserves flow without a full re-derivation. |
| **explain-why** | Keep the restatement, add a sentence on *why it reappears here*. | The repeat is pedagogically deliberate but currently looks accidental. |
| **add-context** | Keep + extend with a genuinely new angle/term. | The "duplicate" actually advances the object (it earns its keep). |
| **rewrite-section** | Restructure the local section to remove internal duplication. | Duplication is *within* one chapter. |
| **rewrite-wider** | Cross-chapter restructuring / move material between chapters. | Two chapters overlap so much their division of labor must change. |

Guiding principle (your concern about "barren references"): **prefer `reminder` over `simple-reference`** wherever the object is load-bearing for the local argument. A bare "(see Ch. 19)" breaks flow; a one-clause reminder + ref keeps the reader moving. Reserve `simple-reference` for objects the reader only *might* want to re-check, and `use-as-known` for the immediately-preceding chapter.
